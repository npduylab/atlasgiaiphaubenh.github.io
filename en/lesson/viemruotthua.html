Acute Appendicitis

Acute appendicitis is a common and urgent surgical condition characterized by the inflammation of the appendix, a finger-like pouch attached to the large intestine. Below is a detailed overview suitable for medical students studying Pathology.

Definition / General

Acute appendicitis is the acute inflammation of the appendix, typically resulting from obstruction of the appendiceal lumen. It is a clinical condition that requires prompt diagnosis and often surgical intervention to prevent complications such as perforation or abscess formation.

Essential Features

  • Sudden onset of abdominal pain, initially periumbilical, then migrating to the right lower quadrant
  • Accompanied by fever, nausea, and sometimes vomiting
  • Leukocytosis (increased white blood cells count) is common
  • Diagnosis primarily clinical but supported by imaging and laboratory tests
  • Histologically, characterized by neutrophilic infiltration of the appendiceal wall

Epidemiology

The term "acute appendicitis" refers specifically to the sudden inflammation of the appendix, distinguishing it from other types of appendiceal pathology such as chronic appendicitis or neoplastic processes.

Epidemiology

Acute appendicitis is one of the most common causes of acute abdominal pain requiring surgery worldwide. It can occur at any age but is most prevalent in the second and third decades of life. There is a slight male predominance.

Pathophysiology

The pathogenesis typically involves obstruction of the appendiceal lumen by fecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, foreign bodies, or tumors, leading to increased intraluminal pressure, decreased blood flow, and subsequent bacterial invasion and inflammation.

Clinical Features

Symptoms start with dull, aching pain in the central abdomen or around the navel, which then shifts to sharp pain in the lower right abdomen. This pain typically worsens with movement, coughing, or other jarring actions. Anorexia, nausea, and vomiting may also be present.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical, based on history and physical examination findings such as McBurney's point tenderness. Laboratory tests and imaging studies like ultrasound and CT scans are used to support the diagnosis and rule out other conditions.

Laboratory

Typical findings include leukocytosis with a left shift. C-reactive protein (CRP) levels may also be elevated.

Radiology Description

Ultrasound and CT can show signs of appendicitis such as an enlarged appendix and peri-appendiceal fat stranding.

Gross Description

The gross pathology of acute appendicitis may reveal an enlarged, erythematous, and possibly gangrenous appendix. Periappendiceal fat may appear inflamed, and in severe cases, there may be evidence of perforation or abscess formation

Microscopic (Histologic) Description

Histologically, acute appendicitis is characterized by transmural acute inflammation, predominantly neutrophilic infiltration, with edema, congestion, and occasionally hemorrhage within the appendiceal wall. Ulceration of the mucosa and fibrinopurulent exudate may be present.
