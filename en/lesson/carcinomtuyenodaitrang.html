Adenocarcinoma of the colon

Adenocarcinoma of the colon, a predominant subtype of colorectal cancer, represents a significant health challenge worldwide. This detailed overview caters to medical professionals, particularly those specializing in surgical pathology and students in the Pathology course. The information provided is comprehensive, covering various aspects from definition to diagnostic procedures, aiming to enhance understanding and clinical skills in diagnosing and managing this condition.

Definition / General

Adenocarcinoma of the colon is a malignant tumor originating from the epithelial cells lining the colon. It typically arises from precancerous polyps through a series of genetic alterations affecting normal cell growth and differentiation.

Essential Features

  • Predominantly affects the lining of the colon.
  • Arises from adenomatous polyps in a significant number of cases.
  • Exhibits a range of histologic patterns, with variable differentiation.

Terminology

The term "adenocarcinoma" refers specifically to a carcinoma that originates in glandular tissue or shows glandular structure. It is distinguished from other types of colorectal cancer by its histological appearance.

Epidemiology

Adenocarcinoma of the colon is one of the leading causes of cancer-related deaths worldwide. It is more prevalent in developed countries, with diet, age, genetic factors, and certain medical conditions (such as inflammatory bowel disease) being significant risk factors.

Pathophysiology

The pathogenesis involves the transformation of normal colonic epithelium through a series of genetic mutations that lead to adenomatous polyps. These polyps can then progress to adenocarcinoma. Key genetic pathways include the APC/beta-catenin pathway, the microsatellite instability pathway, and the CpG island methylator phenotype pathway.

Clinical Features

Symptoms are often nonspecific and may include changes in bowel habits, rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, weight loss, and fatigue. The presentation can vary significantly, depending on the tumor's location within the colon

Diagnosis

The diagnosis is primarily made through colonoscopy and biopsy, allowing for direct visualization and histopathological examination of the tumor.

Laboratory

Blood tests may reveal anemia (due to chronic blood loss) and elevated carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) levels, although CEA is not specific for colon cancer.

Radiology Description

Imaging modalities such as CT scans and MRI are used for staging and assessing the extent of disease, showing the tumor's size, location, and potential metastasis.

Gross examination typically reveals a mass that can be either polypoid, ulcerating, or annular, often causing narrowing of the bowel lumen.

Microscopically, adenocarcinoma of the colon shows glandular formation with varying degrees of differentiation. Poorly differentiated tumors have fewer glandular structures and more solid growth patterns. Features such as invasion depth, lymphovascular invasion, and perineural invasion are important prognostic factors.
