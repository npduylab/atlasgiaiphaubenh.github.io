Goiter

Definition / General

A goiter is an enlargement of the thyroid gland, which is located at the base of the neck. This condition can result from various causes, including iodine deficiency, autoimmune diseases, and thyroid nodules. Goiters can be diffuse, affecting the entire thyroid gland, or nodular, involving parts of the gland

Essential Features

  • Enlargement of the thyroid gland
  • May be associated with euthyroidism, hyperthyroidism, or hypothyroidism
  • Can result from iodine deficiency, autoimmune diseases, or nodular thyroid disease
  • Visible or palpable swelling at the base of the neck

Terminology

  • Diffuse goiter: Uniform enlargement of the thyroid gland without nodules
  • Nodular goiter: Enlargement of the thyroid gland with the presence of nodules
  • Endemic goiter: Occurs in geographical areas with iodine deficiency
  • Sporadic goiter: Occurs individually without a clear geographical or familial pattern

    • Epidemiology

    The prevalence of goiter varies globally, largely influenced by dietary iodine intake. Endemic goiter is common in areas with iodine-deficient soils, whereas sporadic goiter may occur anywhere, often influenced by genetic factors and environmental exposure

    Pathophysiology

    The pathogenesis of goiter involves a disruption in thyroid hormone synthesis, leading to thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) hypersecretion, which stimulates thyroid growth. In iodine deficiency, the lack of iodine impairs thyroid hormone production, leading to compensatory glandular hyperplasia and hypertrophy.

    Clinical Features

    Symptoms of adenomyosis can vary widely, ranging from asymptomatic to severe, including

    • Asymptomatic swelling of the neck
    • Symptoms related to thyroid dysfunction (hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism)
    • Compression symptoms: dysphagia, dyspnea, or hoarseness

    Diagnosis

    Diagnosis is based on clinical examination, thyroid function tests (TFTs), and imaging studies. Thyroid ultrasound is particularly useful for evaluating the gland's structure and detecting nodules.

    Laboratory

    TFTs assess the functional status of the thyroid (TSH, free T4, and sometimes T3 levels). Autoantibody levels may be measured in cases suspected of autoimmune thyroid disease.

    Radiology Description

    Ultrasound can show gland size, echotexture, and nodularity. CT or MRI may be used to assess the extent of large goiters, particularly those extending substernally.

    Diffuse goiter: Uniform enlargement, smooth surface

    Nodular goiter: Irregular surface with nodules of varying sizes; some may contain cystic areas

    Microscopic (Histologic) Description

    • Diffuse goiter: Thyroid follicles vary in size and shape, with some showing colloid depletion and others colloid abundance
    • Nodular goiter: Shows hyperplastic and involutional changes with follicles of varying sizes, some containing dense colloid, and others lined by flattened or hyperplastic epithelium. Areas of fibrosis, calcification, or hemorrhage may be present.
