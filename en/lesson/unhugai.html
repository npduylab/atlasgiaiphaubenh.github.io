Skin papillomas

Skin papillomas, commonly known as skin tags or warts, are benign epithelial tumors that can occur on any part of the body but are most commonly found on the skin. They are characterized by the proliferation of epithelial cells, leading to the formation of a small, often pedunculated (stalk-like) lesion

Definition / General

Skin papillomas are benign, often pedunculated lesions that arise from the epidermis or mucous membranes. They result from the proliferation of keratinocytes and are commonly associated with human papillomavirus (HPV) infections, particularly in the case of anogenital warts.

Essential Features

  • Benign epithelial tumors of the skin or mucous membranes.
  • Often associated with HPV infections, especially types 6 and 11 for anogenital warts
  • Can vary in size from a few millimeters to several centimeters.
  • Typically asymptomatic but can be cosmetically concerning or cause discomfort depending on their location

Terminology

  • Commonly referred to as skin tags, warts, or acrochordons
  • Specific types include verruca vulgaris (common warts), verruca plana (flat warts), and condyloma acuminatum (anogenital warts).

Epidemiology

  • Extremely common with a variable prevalence depending on age, immune status, and geographical location.
  • Anogenital warts are among the most common sexually transmitted infections globally.

Pathophysiology

  • HPV infects keratinocytes, leading to their proliferation and the eventual development of a papilloma
  • The virus integrates into the host DNA, affecting cell cycle regulation and promoting cell growth

Clinical Features

  • Appear as small, flesh-colored, pedunculated or sessile growths on the skin or mucous membranes.
  • Commonly found on the neck, axillae, groin, and eyelids for skin tags; hands and feet for common warts; and anogenital region for condyloma acuminatum.
  • Usually asymptomatic but can become irritated or painful if subjected to trauma.

Diagnosis

  • Primarily clinical, based on the appearance and location of the lesions
  • Dermatoscopy can aid in distinguishing papillomas from other skin lesions
  • Biopsy and histological examination are definitive for uncertain cases.

Laboratory

  • Histopathological examination confirms the diagnosis.
  • PCR and other molecular techniques can identify specific HPV types, particularly for anogenital warts.

Radiology Description

  • Not typically applicable as skin papillomas are diagnosed clinically and histologically.

Gross Description

  • Small, often pedunculated lesions with a soft, velvety, or rough surface.
  • Color can range from skin-toned to hyperpigmented.

Microscopic (Histologic) Description

  • Characterized by acanthosis (thickening of the epidermis), hyperkeratosis (increased keratin), and papillomatosis (proliferation of the papillary dermis).
  • Koilocytes (epithelial cells with perinuclear clearing and nuclear irregularities) are indicative of HPV involvement
