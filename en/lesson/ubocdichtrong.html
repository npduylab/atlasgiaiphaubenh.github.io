Serous Cystadenoma

Definition / General

Serous cystadenoma is a type of benign tumor that predominantly occurs in the pancreas, although it can also arise in the ovaries and other organs. These tumors are characterized by the presence of numerous small, fluid-filled cysts lined by a single layer of benign epithelial cells. They are typically non-invasive and have a low potential for malignancy.

Essential Features

  • Benign cystic tumor composed of numerous small cysts containing serous fluid.
  • Lined by cuboidal, serous epithelial cells with clear cytoplasm.
  • Generally asymptomatic and discovered incidentally
  • Very low potential for malignancy

Terminology

The term "serous cystadenoma" reflects the serous (clear, watery) nature of the fluid within the cysts and the glandular origin of the tumor (adenoma).

Epidemiology

Serous cystadenomas are more common in middle-aged and elderly individuals, with a higher prevalence in women. The pancreatic form is relatively rare, comprising about 1-2% of all pancreatic cystic lesions.

Pathophysiology

The exact cause of serous cystadenoma is unknown. These tumors are believed to arise from epithelial cells lining ducts or glands. Genetic factors may play a role in their development, but environmental or lifestyle factors have not been firmly established as risk factors.

Clinical Features

Most serous cystadenomas are asymptomatic and are discovered incidentally during imaging studies for unrelated reasons. When symptoms do occur, they may include abdominal pain, nausea, or a palpable mass, depending on the tumor's size and location.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is typically made through imaging studies such as ultrasound, computed tomography (CT) scans, or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). These tests can help to characterize the lesion's cystic nature and its relationship with surrounding structures.

Laboratory

There are no specific laboratory tests for diagnosing serous cystadenoma. Blood tests may be performed to rule out other conditions

Radiology Description

Radiologically, serous cystadenomas appear as well-circumscribed, microcystic lesions. They may show a central scar with radiating septa in some cases, especially in the pancreas.

Gross Description

On gross examination, serous cystadenomas are typically multilocular, with numerous small cysts that contain clear, straw-colored fluid. The external surface is usually smooth.

Microscopic (Histologic) Description

Microscopically, serous cystadenomas are characterized by cysts lined by a single layer of flat or cuboidal epithelial cells with clear, glycogen-rich cytoplasm. The nuclei are centrally located and typically benign in appearance. There is usually no significant atypia or mitotic activity.
