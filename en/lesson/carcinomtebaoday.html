Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common type of skin cancer, originating from the basal cells of the epidermis. It is characterized by its slow growth and minimal tendency to metastasize, though it can cause significant local destruction if not treated appropriately. This overview covers the essential aspects of basal cell carcinoma relevant to pathology studies.

Definition / General

Basal cell carcinoma is a malignant epithelial skin tumor with cells resembling those of the basal layer of the epidermis. It primarily arises in sun-exposed areas of the skin, particularly in individuals with fair skin

Essential Features

  • Most common form of skin cancer
  • Originates from basal cells of the epidermis
  • Characterized by slow growth and rare metastasis
  • High recurrence risk if not completely excised

Terminology

BCC is also known colloquially as a rodent ulcer when it becomes ulcerative.

Epidemiology

BCC is most prevalent in populations with lighter skin types and increases with age. The incidence has been rising, likely due to increased sun exposure and longer life expectancies.

Pathophysiology

BCC originates from mutations in the basal cell layer of the epidermis, often involving the Hedgehog signaling pathway. DNA damage induced by ultraviolet (UV) radiation is a key factor in its pathogenesis.

Clinical Features

  • Often appears as a pearly or waxy bump on sun-exposed skin, such as the face, ears, and neck.
  • May present as a flat, scaly, flesh-colored or brown patch on the back or chest
  • Ulceration and bleeding may occur in advanced stages.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily based on clinical examination and confirmed by histopathological analysis of a skin biopsy.

Laboratory

Not typically applicable for BCC, as diagnosis and assessment are primarily histological.

Radiology Description

Radiology is not a standard diagnostic tool for BCC, but advanced imaging techniques may be used to assess deep tissue invasion in extensive cases

Gross Description

Grossly, BCCs may appear as nodular lesions, often with a pearly edge and telangiectasia. Ulceration may be present in more advanced lesions.

Histologically, BCC is characterized by nests of basaloid cells with palisading nuclei at the periphery. These cells exhibit minimal cytoplasm and high nuclear-cytoplasmic ratios. Several histological subtypes exist, including nodular, superficial, morpheaform (sclerosing), and infiltrative, each with distinct microscopic features.
