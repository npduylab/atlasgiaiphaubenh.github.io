Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is a primary malignancy of the liver, arising predominantly from hepatocytes, the liver's main cell type. It represents the most common type of liver cancer worldwide and is a leading cause of cancer-related deaths. The complexity of HCC encompasses its varied etiology, pathogenesis, clinical presentation, and histological features, making it a significant focus in the field of surgical pathology.

Definition / General

Hepatocellular carcinoma is defined as a malignant tumor that originates from hepatocytes. It is characterized by its aggressive behavior, potential for widespread metastasis, and association with chronic liver disease and cirrhosis.

Essential Features

  • Primary liver cancer originating from hepatocytes
  • Strongly associated with chronic liver diseases, particularly those leading to cirrhosis
  • Variable clinical presentation, from asymptomatic to decompensated liver disease

Terminology

The term "hepatocellular carcinoma" is used to specifically describe malignancies derived from hepatocytes, distinguishing it from other liver cancers such as cholangiocarcinoma (arising from bile duct cells).

Epidemiology

HCC has a high prevalence in regions with endemic hepatitis B and C virus infections. It is more common in males than females and typically affects adults. Its incidence varies globally, with the highest rates in East Asia and sub-Saharan Africa.

Pathophysiology

The development of HCC is a multistep process often arising in the context of chronic liver inflammation and cirrhosis. Key factors include genetic mutations, oxidative stress, and the activation of oncogenic pathways. Chronic infection with hepatitis B or C virus is a major risk factor, along with aflatoxin exposure, alcohol abuse, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease.

Clinical Features

Symptoms of HCC may include weight loss, abdominal pain, jaundice, and ascites. Many patients are asymptomatic in the early stages, with tumors often discovered incidentally during imaging studies for other reasons

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of HCC involves a combination of clinical evaluation, imaging techniques (such as ultrasound, CT, and MRI), and biopsy for histological examination. Serum biomarkers, including alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), can aid in diagnosis and monitoring

Laboratory

Elevated liver enzymes and AFP levels may be indicative of HCC. Other tests assess liver function and the presence of viral hepatitis.

Radiology Description

Radiological features of HCC include a hypervascular mass with arterial enhancement and venous or delayed phase washout on contrast-enhanced studies. The use of specific imaging criteria (e.g., LI-RADS) helps in the diagnosis and staging of HCC.

Gross Description

Grossly, HCC can present as a solitary mass, multifocal nodules, or diffuse infiltration of the liver. The tumors may vary in color from tan to green, depending on the presence of bile, and can have areas of hemorrhage or necrosis.

Histologically, HCC exhibits a variety of patterns, including trabecular, pseudoglandular, and solid. Tumor cells resemble hepatocytes but show pleomorphism, increased nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio, and abnormal mitotic figures. Vascular invasion is a common finding.

Reference

  • "WHO Classification of Tumours of the Digestive System," which provides detailed histological classification and diagnostic criteria for HCC and other liver tumors.
  • Current literature and guidelines from the American Association for the Study of Liver Diseases (AASLD) and the European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL) for the latest consensus on management and treatment of HCC.
